Here it is: Once upon a time, high up in the Whispering Mountains, there lived a tiny dragon named Pip. Now, Pip was a perfectly lovely dragon in almost every way. He had shimmery green scales that caught the moonlight like little mirrors. He had a tail that curled up at the end like a question mark. He had enormous golden eyes that were very good at looking adorable — which came in handy when he'd eaten someone's pie by accident. But there was one thing Pip could not do. He could not breathe fire. Not even a little bit. Not even a tiny spark. Instead of fire, what came out of Pip when he tried to breathe flames was... a small puff of smoke that smelled exactly like burnt toast. And then a hiccup. Every morning, all the other dragons in the village would do their morning stretches — flap, flap, WHOOOOSH — great big beautiful flames that lit up the sky in brilliant oranges and reds. And every morning, Pip would stretch, flap, flap... PFFT. Burnt toast smell. Then a hiccup. "Don't worry, Pip," said his mum, patting him on the head. "You'll get there." "HOW?" said Pip, because he really wanted to know. His mum had no idea. She just smiled the way mums do when they don't actually have an answer but want to seem like they do. So Pip decided he needed to find someone who DID have an answer. 🐌 Chapter 2: The World's Slowest Wise Person Pip trudged down the mountain, past the sparkling river, through the Gigglebush Forest (where the bushes actually giggled — it was a bit weird), and arrived at a mossy log. On the mossy log sat a snail. The snail was extremely, magnificently, impressively slow. In fact, it took the snail about four minutes just to turn around to look at Pip. Pip waited politely, tapping his claws on the ground. "Ah," said the snail, finally. "A dragon." "Hello," said Pip. "Are you wise?" "I am," said the snail. "My name is Gerald. Though I prefer to be called Gerald the Magnificent, but most people just say Gerald because they run out of patience." "I can't breathe fire," said Pip. "Can you help me?" Gerald looked at Pip for a very long time. This was partly because he was wise and thinking deeply. And partly because blinking took Gerald approximately six seconds per blink. "Hmm," said Gerald, finally. "Tell me, Pip. What do you do every day when you wake up?" Pip thought about it. "I wake up. I look at the other dragons doing their big whooshy flames. I feel sad. I try once, go PFFT, feel MORE sad, and then go eat breakfast." "And what do you eat?" "Mushroom porridge. With a blueberry on top if it's a good day." "And after breakfast?" "I... go back to bed a bit. Then maybe play. Then try the fire thing again. Another PFFT. More sadness. Then lunch." Gerald blinked. It took six seconds. "I see," he said. "Pip, my boy. You are making a very common dragon mistake." "What's that?" said Pip. "You are waiting," said Gerald, "to become a fire-breather. Instead of already being one." Pip blinked. His blinks were much faster than Gerald's, which felt like a personal victory. "What does that even MEAN?" Gerald smiled in the slow, smug way that snails who are very wise tend to smile. "Sit down," he said. "This is going to take a while." 🧊 Chapter 3: The Secret of the Ice Cube Gerald told Pip a story within the story, which Pip found very exciting because stories inside stories are like presents inside presents. Three. Two. One. Nothing, nothing, nothing." "This is a very boring story," said Pip honestly. "Zero degrees," said Gerald, ignoring him. "AND THEN — " "WHAT." "Nothing. The ice cube still just sits there, being cold." Pip stared at Gerald. "Gerald. GERALD. I need you to get to the point." "But then," said Gerald, and here he did a dramatic pause that was at least three weeks long, "zero point ONE degrees. And the whole ice cube melts. ALL at once." Pip looked at Gerald. Gerald looked at Pip. "Okay," said Pip. "But what does that mean for ME and my fire problem?" "All that warmth that came before — all of it mattered," said Gerald. Gerald explained that one percent didn't mean trying a huge fire blast and getting a slightly bigger PFFT. It meant TINY, TINY steps. Almost embarrassingly tiny. For Pip, tiny meant this: every morning, after his mushroom porridge, he would practice feeling the warmth in his tummy. Just that. Not breathing it out. Just... noticing it was there. "That's IT?" said Pip. "That's the practice? Just NOTICING?" "For now," said Gerald. "That seems too easy." "It IS easy. That's the point. We need it to be easy enough that you'll actually DO it.
